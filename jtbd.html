Food Scheduling: From Friction to On-Demand Plus

Understanding why users schedule food orders—and why they actively avoid it. A behavioral product analysis.

Executive Summary

Food delivery apps offer scheduling features, yet adoption remains surprisingly low. My analysis reveals this is not a feature problem — it is a behavioral problem. Users choose not to schedule because scheduling feels like losing control.

💡

The Solution ('On-Demand Plus'): Restructure the interaction model so users always order now (preserving instant-order psychology), choose when delivery arrives (preserving control), and can adjust anytime penalty-free.

The Deep JTBD Framework

While functional jobs are easily satisfied by current apps, the emotional and social jobs reveal deep, unaddressed anxieties around commitment and accountability.

First Principles: Why Scheduling Fails

By deconstructing the problem from first principles, loss of control is the primary barrier. Users prefer 'Present-me' making decisions over 'Past-me' making decisions.

💡

When a user schedules, they lose Temporal Control, Preference Control, Social Control, and Psychological Control — all simultaneously.
