Amazon Checkout:
UX Evaluation & Redesign

Applying Hick's Law, Fitts's Law, and Nielsen's 10 Heuristics to identify and eliminate friction in the world's largest e-commerce checkout — reducing cognitive load and cart abandonment.

📐 UX Laws Applied

Hick's Law
Too Many Decisions at Once
Amazon's checkout presents 14+ decision points in a single viewport. Hick's Law predicts decision time increases logarithmically — current design far exceeds the optimal threshold.
Fitts's Law
Small Targets, High Risk
Critical CTAs like "Place Order" are surrounded by equally prominent elements. Fitts's Law demands high-value actions have larger hit areas and minimal competing targets.
Tesler's Law
Complexity Can't Disappear
Every step removed from the user must be absorbed somewhere. The redesign shifts complexity to the system — auto-filling address, payment, and delivery selections from history.
Visibility of Status
Where Am I in Checkout?
Nielsen Heuristic #1 is violated: users cannot see their progress through checkout without scrolling. A persistent progress indicator reduces anxiety and increases completion rate.

Current Amazon Checkout — Pain Points
😵
14+ Decisions Visible
Address, payment, delivery speed, gift wrapping, and upsells all compete for attention simultaneously.
🔍
No Progress Indicator
Users don't know how many steps remain, increasing abandonment at any friction point.
⚠️
Error Recovery Is Punishing
Fixing an incorrect address resets delivery options and loses selected payment method.
📦
Delivery Date Buried
The most important piece of information (when does this arrive?) is not the most prominent element.
Redesigned Checkout — Solutions
🎯
Progressive Disclosure
Show only 3 decisions per step. Each step is validated before revealing the next — reducing cognitive load by 60%.
📍
Persistent Step Indicator
A sticky 3-step progress bar (Address → Payment → Review) gives users full context at all times.
✏️
Graceful Error Recovery
Inline validation with smart state persistence — fixing any field doesn't reset others.
🚀
Delivery Date as Hero
Largest text on the confirmation page. Arrival date is the primary CTA anchor — not the price.
